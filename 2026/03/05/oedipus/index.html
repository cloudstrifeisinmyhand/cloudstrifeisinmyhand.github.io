'Do not go gentle into that good night': Analysis on Oedipus' behavioral ethics from the perspective of free will

In Sophocles’ Oedipus Rex, the tragic hero Oedipus accidentally kills his biological father, whom he has never known, even though he is trying to avoid the prophecy of killing his father. The act of killing the father is unethical, but Oedipus is manipulated by fate into committing an incestuous act while avoiding the incestuous act in advance. Therefore, the ethics of Oedipus’ actions cannot be simply pinned down. This essay will first introduce the concept of free will and the compatibility paradox, then analyze Oedipus’ virtues, actions and roles from the angle of how free will impact on moral responsibility, and finally proposed an ethical acceptance of Oedipus’s act of resisting with foreknowledge of destiny.

According to Jin (2023), Kant’s quote provides a widely accepted definition of free will, addressing that if a person takes the necessary action of their own will, then they are acting freely. Sophocles differed from his predecessor, Aeschylus, in that he gave his characters more free will than simply being manipulated by fate. However, Oedipus, the tragic hero who is committed to rebelling against the oracle, still does not escape being manipulated by fate in the story. Therefore, another important concept that needs to be introduced is determinism as embodied in the story. Determinism holds that all events in the universe, including human decisions and actions, are causally unavoidable. In this way, free will and determinism constitute the paradox known as the Compatibility Problem, the central contradiction of which is the discussion of whether human beings can still have free will and moral responsibility if determinism is valid. The dominant views that have been put forward on this issue range from Compatibilism to many sorts of Incompatibilism. In response to the inconsistencies, some scholars have argued that determinism, with its focus on “must be”, and fatalism, with its focus on “irresistibility”, have been conflated in Western academic discussions, rendering the discussion of compatibilism in Oedipus’ tragedy null and void (Liu, 2017). Furthermore, Chen (2022) argues that Sophocles is using free will to mask the deterministic nature of the story and that Oedipus does not possess free will at all. However, commenting on an Oedipus with no free will detracts from the real value of reading the story, so the following argument will avoid a discussion of the paradox itself, defaulting to the idea that Oedipus possesses free will in each particular situation, and thus assessing the morality of Oedipus’ behavior.

Since the tragedy of Oedipus is a work of literature, the author’s tone largely determines what extent to the free will of Oedipus influences his moral responsibility. In the story, Oedipus chooses to leave Corinth out of his own free will in order to avoid killing his father, and describes himself as “turning his back on the land of Corinth and fleeing to a place where he could never see the prophecy”, which reflects Oedipus’ moral intention. However, Oedipus’ biological father, Laius, attempts to kill Oedipus three days after he is born after learning of the oracle that he will be killed by his son. By portraying a more selfish father, Sophocles emphasizes Oedipus’ morality. In Oedipus’ confession about the three road plot, a herald and the man in the carriage “rudely asked Oedipus to pull over,” so Oedipus, in a fit of rage, struck the driver of the carriage who shoved him. The old man in the carriage then took advantage of Oedipus’ passing by to pick up a “two-pointed club” from the carriage and bring it down on Oedipus’ head, but Oedipus fought back and killed him. According to Kitto’s commentary, Sophocles portrays this as enough to save Oedipus from serious culpability: if Oedipus had not struck quickly, he would have been killed by his father, and the arrogance of the conflict rests solely on the coachman and Laius (Sommerstein, 2013). This shows that in the story, even though he strikes out of anger, it is also the father who first tries to kill him when he is about to leave, and he kills out of self-defense in the nick of time to a much greater extent. There is little room left for Oedipus’ free will here, and the weight of moral responsibility is diminished. Another key point in the story is the effect of agonia on the relationship with moral responsibility. “Agnoia” refers to a lack of knowledge or understanding, especially in a spiritual or moral context. Based on the description of the story, it is easy to learn that Oedipus has agnoia about the fact. Thus, Oedipus’ actions lack subjective malice and he even acts on his own initiative to avoid the prophecy. According to Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics, moral responsibility requires two conditions to be met, namely, that the act is voluntary and that the actor has knowledge of the nature of the act. Although Oedipus’ patricide is voluntary, his lack of knowledge of the object and consequences of the act makes it difficult to assess his moral responsibility. In addition, after learning the truth, Oedipus blinds himself and exiles himself, which is an assumption of responsibility and a confrontation of guilt, and therefore moral.

Moreover, in addition to each individual act of Oedipus, it is worth judging the act of “resistance” itself, which Oedipus chooses to avoid with all his might despite knowing the end of his fate. In the scenario of “knowing the end but trying to resist it”, the result of the act of resistance is placed in the extreme case of “having known it”, and therefore choosing to resist it is the greatest use of free will and the greatest assumption of responsibility. From a utilitarian point of view, this act of resistance seems meaningless, but from the point of view of virtue ethics and deontology, this act of resistance is in line with the virtues of courage, hope, and dignity, and does not negate the subjectivity of human beings because of the result, so it is moral. At the same time, Oedipus’ act of defiance has the nature of pursuing truth. He solves the mystery of the Sphinx in the story, which reflects that he is a truth-seeker, and his commitment to avoiding prophecy also reflects that he does not accept blind faith in unproven events, but prefers to use his subjective initiative to pursue the truth. In addition, the eyes and the act of seeing are recurring themes in the story, and vision symbolizes knowledge and self-awareness. For the development of mankind, the choice to resist fate in an atmosphere of hopelessness is an inspiring virtue, and this is the greatest value of Oedipus as a fictional character, which is celebrated in Dylan Thomas’s poem: “Do not go gentle into the good night. good night, when day shall end, and twilight shall still burn and roar; rage, rage against the dying of the light.”

In conclusion, in combination with the subjective will of Sophocles in creating the character of Oedipus, Oedipus’s actions, including the killing of his father, are moral when viewed through the lens of free will. More importantly, Oedipus’ act of defiance against the oracle exemplifies the valuable virtue of human beings’ courage to maximize their free will in desperate situations.
